Moving Beyond "Good Food" and "Bad Food"

By Daniela Mondello APD · Accredited Practising Dietitian & Nutritionist

Food guilt is one of the most damaging things I encounter in my clinic. It's also one of the most common. And much of it stems from a simple but deeply ingrained habit: labelling food as "good" or "bad."

Why we do it

Moral food labelling is everywhere — in diet culture, social media, well-meaning health messaging, and even the way doctors sometimes talk to patients. We're told to "be good" at dinner, to "cheat" on weekends, to "earn" dessert. Over time, eating a "bad" food doesn't just feel like a nutritional choice — it feels like a personal failure.

What actually happens when we label food as "bad"

The research on this is consistent and sobering. When we restrict foods we've labelled as "off limits," we tend to think about them more. When we eventually eat them, we often overeat — because we feel like we've already failed, so we might as well go all the way. Then comes the guilt, the restriction, the cycle repeating. This is sometimes called the "what the hell" effect, and it's one of the key drivers of disordered eating patterns.

What a healthier approach actually looks like

This doesn't mean all foods are nutritionally equal — they're not. Some foods nourish our bodies more than others, and that's worth knowing. But nourishment is just one dimension of eating. Food is also pleasure, culture, connection, comfort and celebration. A piece of birthday cake at your child's party is not a failure. A bowl of pasta with your family on Sunday is not a setback.

In my clinic I encourage clients to think about:

  • Eating mostly wholefoods — not because other foods are "bad," but because they make your body feel good
  • Noticing how foods make you feel — energy, digestion, mood — rather than assigning moral value
  • Allowing all foods unconditionally — paradoxically, this often reduces cravings and the urge to binge
  • Practising curiosity rather than judgment — asking "am I hungry? What do I actually want?" rather than "should I be eating this?"

When to seek extra support

If food guilt is significantly affecting your quality of life, your relationship with eating or your mental health, please reach out. I work with clients on these patterns alongside the nutritional side, and refer to clinical psychologists when additional support is needed. You don't have to navigate this alone.
